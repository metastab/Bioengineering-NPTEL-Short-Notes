### Overview of Biotechnology Applications

Modern biology is shifting toward "omics" (e.g., genomics and proteomics), which involves studying all the biomolecules of an organism as a whole system rather than one gene or protein at a time.

  * **Gene Cloning and Mutants**: DNA cloning techniques allow scientists to study mutant genes or proteins by introducing sequence changes at the **primer designing level** during a **Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)**.
  * **Recombinant DNA**: A gene of interest is fused with a **plasmid vector** to create a **recombinant DNA molecule**, which is then moved into a host (like bacteria) through **transformation**.
  * **Protein Production**: Cloning and gene expression systems are used to produce large quantities of specific proteins for pharmaceutical use.
      * **Histidine Tags**: Researchers can add **6-histidine tags** to a gene sequence during primer design.
      * **Affinity Chromatography**: These tagged proteins can be purified using columns filled with **Nickel NTA** resins, which specifically bind to histidine.
  * **Pharmaceutical Examples**:
      * **Human Insulin and HGH**: Mass-produced for millions of people with diabetes or growth hormone deficiencies.
      * **Tissue Plasminogen Activator (TPA)**: Administered after heart attacks to dissolve blood clots.
      * **Drug Targets**: Identifying small molecules, such as the inhibitor **Imatinib**, which blocks target proteins like **BCR-ABL** to treat chronic myelogenous leukemia (CML).
  * **Transgenic "Pharm" Animals**: Organisms like goats can be genetically modified to secrete human blood proteins, such as **anti-thrombin**, in their milk for therapeutic use.

### DNA Sequencing Technologies

The document outlines the history and technical progression of determining an organism's genetic code.

  * **Human Genome Project**: A massive international effort started in 1990 that aimed to sequence the roughly 20,300 human genes. A draft was published in 2001, revealing that the number of genes was lower than expected and relatively uniform across different organisms.
  * **Sanger Method (1st Generation)**: Developed by Frederick Sanger, this method involves denaturing DNA into single strands and using a primer with DNA polymerase, **deoxyribonucleotides (dNTPs)**, and **dideoxyribonucleotides (ddNTPs)**.
      * **Chain Termination**: ddNTPs lack a **3'OH group**, which terminates the growing DNA strand upon incorporation.
      * **Fluorescent Labeling**: Each ddNTP is fluorescently tagged, allowing the terminated fragments to be read by **fluorescent scanners** to determine the sequence.
  * **Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS)**:
      * **2nd Generation**: Includes techniques like **pyro-sequencing** and **reversible chain termination**.
      * **3rd Generation**: Focuses on lowering costs and increasing speed using physical principles like **protein nanopore technology**, **FRET** (fluorescence resonance energy transfer), and **single-molecule detection**.
  * **Impact**: These advancements have reduced the cost and time of genome sequencing significantly, moving from a billion-dollar, 15-year project to a process that can take 2–3 days and cost approximately 50,000 to 60,000 rupees.

#### Short Question Answers

1.  Transgenic "pharm" animals are organisms modified to produce human proteins for therapeutic use. For example, a goat can be engineered with a trait for a human blood protein, like **anti-thrombin**, which is then secreted in its milk and can be purified to prevent blood clots in patients.
2.  The Human Genome Project found that the total number of human genes was much lower than expected and that gene numbers are relatively uniform across different organisms.
3.  In Sanger sequencing, ddNTPs are added to the reaction along with standard dNTPs. Because ddNTPs lack a **3'OH group**, their incorporation terminates DNA strand synthesis. Each ddNTP is fluorescently tagged with a specific color, allowing a scanner to identify the terminating base (A, T, G, or C) at the end of each fragment.
4.  Physical principles and engineering have dramatically accelerated sequencing speed and reduced costs. Techniques like protein nanopore and FRET technology allow for faster reading of bases, reducing a project that once took 15 years and billions of dollars into one that takes 2–3 days and costs roughly 50,000 to 60,000 rupees.
5.  A recombinant DNA molecule is created by fusing a gene of interest with a **plasmid vector**. This molecule is then introduced into a bacterial host through **transformation**, where the bacteria can multiply to create many copies of the gene or be used for protein production.
