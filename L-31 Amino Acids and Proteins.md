This document, titled **"Amino Acids & Proteins"** (Lecture 31 of the course *Bioengineering: An Interface with Biology and Medicine*), focuses on the fundamental concepts of protein structure and function. Its primary purpose is to introduce the building blocks of proteins—amino acids—and explain how their diverse properties lead to the complex structural levels necessary for biological functionality.

### Why Studying Proteins is Crucial

Proteins are vital to study because they perform a vast range of cellular functions and accurately reflect human physiology.

  * **Physiological Reflection**: Proteins most accurately represent what is occurring inside the human body.
  * **Disease and Drugs**: Many diseases result from **protein malfunction** (failure of a protein to perform its intended role), and most current drugs either target protein functions or are proteins themselves.
  * **Central Dogma**: In the flow of genetic information, **transcription** (DNA to RNA) and **translation** (RNA to protein) ultimately lead to functional proteins.

### Amino Acids: The Building Blocks

Amino acids are the **monomeric units** (single basic molecules) of polypeptides and proteins.

  * **General Structure**: Every amino acid consists of a central **alpha ($\\alpha$) carbon** bonded to an **amino group** ($NH\_2$), a **carboxyl group** ($COOH$), a hydrogen atom, and a variable **side chain** ($R$ group).
  * **Standard Set**: There are 20 standard amino acids, each identified by unique three-letter and single-letter codes.
  * **Essential vs. Non-essential**: **Essential amino acids** cannot be synthesized by the organism and must be obtained through diet, whereas **non-essential amino acids** can be synthesized from various precursors.

### Classification of Amino Acids

Amino acids are primarily classified based on the properties of their side chains ($R$ groups).

  * **Non-polar Aliphatic (Hydrophobic)**: These have hydrocarbon side chains and include **Glycine** (the simplest, **achiral**—meaning it lacks a non-superimposable mirror image), **Alanine**, **Valine**, **Leucine**, **Isoleucine**, and **Methionine** (which contains sulfur). **Proline** is unique because it has a ring structure that provides conformational restriction.
  * **Polar Uncharged (Hydrophilic)**: These are water-soluble and include **Serine**, **Threonine**, **Cysteine** (containing a **sulfhydryl** or thiol group), **Asparagine**, and **Glutamine**.
  * **Electrically Charged (Hydrophilic)**:
      * **Acidic**: Negatively charged side chains, including **Aspartic acid** and **Glutamic acid**.
      * **Basic**: Positively charged side chains, including **Lysine**, **Arginine**, and **Histidine**.
  * **Aromatic**: These contain ring structures and include **Phenylalanine**, **Tyrosine**, and **Tryptophan**. These rings strongly absorb UV light, a characteristic used for protein estimation.

### Properties and Isomerism

  * **Peptide Bond**: Amino acids link through a **condensation reaction** where the carboxyl group of one links to the amino group of another, losing a water molecule. This bond is rigid due to its partial double bond character.
  * **Chirality**: Except for glycine, all amino acids have an asymmetric center, making them **chiral**. They exist as **enantiomers** (non-superimposable mirror images) designated as **D** and **L** isomers. Most proteins found in nature consist of L-configurations.
  * **Acid-Base Properties**: In acidic media, amino acids are **cationic** (positively charged). In highly alkaline media, they are **anionic** (negatively charged). At a specific pH, they have no net charge and exist as a **zwitterion**.

### Levels of Protein Structure

Proteins are organized into four structural levels:

  * **Primary Structure**: The linear sequence of amino acids joined by peptide bonds. It determines the overall 3D structure and tells the evolutionary history of the protein.
  * **Secondary Structure**: Locally folded regions, such as **alpha ($\\alpha$) helices** (rod-like coils) and **beta ($\\beta$) sheets** (extended strands), stabilized by hydrogen bonds. Permissible folding angles are described by the **Ramachandran plot**.
  * **Tertiary Structure**: The overall 3D folding of a single polypeptide, driven by interactions between distant side chains, where hydrophobic residues are typically buried inside.
  * **Quaternary Structure**: The arrangement and interaction of multiple protein subunits (individual polypeptide chains) in a complex.

### Protein Functions

Proteins perform diverse roles, including **enzyme catalysis** (speeding up reactions), **transport and storage** (e.g., oxygen), **coordinated motion** (muscle contraction), **mechanical strength** (e.g., collagen), **immunity** (antibodies), and **signalling**.
