This document, titled **"Evolution,"** is the 29th lecture in the course *Bioengineering: An Interface with Biology and Medicine*. Its primary purpose is to provide a glimpse into Charles Darwin’s theory on the origin of species and to demonstrate how evolutionary concepts are essential for modern biological progress, including the study of virus evolution and cancer progression.

### The Nature and Goals of Science: Why study Evolution?

Studying evolution is essential for biological progress and saving lives.

  * **Virus Evolution**: Tracking the evolution of viruses like **influenza**, **HIV**, and **H1N1** helps scientists understand how these pathogens change over time.
  * **Gene Recombinations**: Evolutionary biologists study how viruses create different strains through **gene recombination** (the exchange of genetic material between different organisms), which allows for quicker decoding of their sequences and the development of prevention methods.
  * **Modern Biology**: Evolutionary concepts are now integrated into the majority of modern biology and biotechnology experiments.

### Facts Known Before Darwin

Before Darwin, several concepts regarding Earth’s history and the life upon it were already recognized.

  * **Ancient Environment and Fossils**: It was known that the Earth’s ancient environment had changed significantly over periods like the Jurassic era, leaving behind **fossils** (the preserved remains or traces of ancient organisms).
  * **Inheritance of Acquired Characteristics**: A scientist named **Lamarck** proposed that organisms could pass on traits acquired during their lifetime to their offspring. He used the example of giraffes evolving long necks because they stretched them higher over many generations to reach food.

### Charles Darwin and the Origin of Species

Charles Darwin (1809-1882) was a naturalist who fundamentally changed biology with his 1859 book, *On the Origin of Species by Means of Natural Selection*.

  * **The Voyage of the Beagle**: From 1831 to 1836, Darwin served as a **Naturalist** (a person who studies plants and animals by observing them) on a survey expedition to South America aboard the **HMS Beagle**.
  * **Evidence and Adaptations**: During this voyage, he collected various birds, insects, plants, and fossils. These experiences led him to believe that new species originate from ancestral forms through the accumulation of **adaptations** (features that help an organism survive and reproduce in its environment).
  * **Galápagos Finches**: Darwin observed that finches on different islands had developed different **beak variations** suited to their specific food sources, such as seeds, insects, or cactus flowers.

### Natural Selection and Descent with Modification

Darwin proposed **natural selection** as the primary mechanism for evolution.

  * **Natural Selection**: This is a process where individuals with certain heritable traits survive and reproduce at a higher rate than others because of those specific traits. Over time, this increases the "match" between organisms and their environment.
  * **Descent with Modification**: Darwin argued that life's diversity arose from ancestral species through this process, which differed from previous theories like Lamarckism.
  * **Artificial Selection**: In contrast to natural selection, **artificial selection** occurs when humans deliberately breed organisms for specific traits, such as deriving various vegetables (like cabbage and broccoli) from wild mustard.

### Convergent vs. Divergent Evolution

Evolution can take different paths depending on the environment and ancestry.

  * **Convergent Evolution**: This occurs when independent species in different environments evolve similar traits, such as the sugar glider in Australia and the flying squirrel in North America.
  * **Divergent Evolution**: This occurs when a trait retained from a common ancestor evolves into different variations in different species. For example, the forelimbs of humans, cats, whales, and bats are anatomically similar but have been adapted for different activities like flying or swimming.

### Testing Evolution: The Hardy-Weinberg Law

The **Hardy-Weinberg Law** is a mathematical model used to test whether a population is evolving at the genetics level.

  * **Key Conditions**: For a population to be in **equilibrium** (not evolving), five conditions must be met: the population must be very large, isolated (no migration), have no mutations, engage in random mating, and experience no natural selection.
  * **The Equation**: The law is expressed as **$p^2 + 2pq + q^2 = 1$**, where:
      * **$p$**: Frequency of the **dominant allele** (the variant of a gene that masks a recessive one).
      * **$q$**: Frequency of the **recessive allele** (the variant of a gene masked by a dominant one).
      * **$p^2$**: Frequency of the **homozygous dominant** genotype.
      * **$q^2$**: Frequency of the **homozygous recessive** genotype.
      * **$2pq$**: Frequency of the **heterozygous** genotype (having two different alleles for a trait).

By using this law, scientists can integrate biological concepts into mathematical models to predict genetic frequencies and inheritance patterns within a population.
