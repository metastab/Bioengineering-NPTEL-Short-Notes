This lecture focuses on the **Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)**, a revolutionary biotechnology tool used to make multiple copies of a specific DNA segment.

### **1. Introduction to PCR**

PCR was discovered in **1984 by Kary Mullis**. It is similar to gene cloning but uses chemical processes to exponentially amplify a target DNA sequence.

  * **Taq Polymerase:** The success of PCR is based on a heat-stable enzyme called **Taq polymerase**.
  * **Source:** It was isolated from the bacterium *Thermus aquaticus*, which lives in hot springs and can survive temperatures up to **95°C**.
  * **Function:** Because it is heat-stable, it does not denature during the high-temperature phases of the PCR cycle.

### **2. The Three Steps of a PCR Cycle**

A single PCR cycle consists of three temperature-dependent steps that result in doubling the target DNA.

  * **Step 1: Denaturation (Strand Separation):** The double-stranded DNA is heated briefly (approx. **95°C–100°C**) to separate it into two single strands.
  * **Step 2: Annealing:** The mixture is cooled to allow **primers** (short nucleotide stretches) to form hydrogen bonds with the ends of the target sequence.
  * **Step 3: Extension (DNA Synthesis):** **Taq polymerase** adds nucleotides to the 3' end of each primer, synthesizing a new DNA strand. This typically occurs around **72°C**.

### **3. Primers and Their Design**

**Primers** are essential starting points for DNA synthesis.

  * **Characteristics:** They are usually **18–28 nucleotides** long with a **50–60% GC composition**.
  * **Melting Temperature (Tm):** This is the specific temperature at which primers bind to the DNA. It is calculated using the formula: $Tm = (A+T) \\times 2 + (G+C) \\times 4$.
  * **Direction:** There are two primers: a **forward primer** (complementary to the lower strand) and a **reverse primer** (complementary to the upper strand). Both are written in the **5' to 3' direction**.

### **4. Applications and Advanced Concepts**

  * **Exponential Growth:** PCR produces an exponentially growing population of identical DNA molecules. The number of molecules equals **$2^n$**, where $n$ is the number of cycles. Typically, **30 to 40 cycles** are performed.
  * **Forensics:** PCR is vital in crime scene investigations to amplify small amounts of DNA from biological specimens like hair or blood for accurate testing.
  * **Mutant Creation:** Researchers can introduce mutations at the primer design level. By changing just one base pair in a triplet codon (e.g., changing TCT for Serine to TGT for Cysteine), they can study how changes at the gene level affect protein function.
  * **Expression Vectors:** To express eukaryotic proteins in bacterial (prokaryotic) systems, specialized **expression vectors** are used. These contain highly active bacterial promoters to ensure the eukaryotic gene is inserted in the correct reading frame for proper protein synthesis.
  * **Thermal Cycler:** The instrument used for this process is a **thermal cycler**, which precisely regulates the rapid temperature changes required for the three steps of PCR.

#### Short-Answer Solutions

1.  **Denaturation:** Heating briefly to separate double-stranded DNA into single strands. **Annealing:** Cooling to allow primers to form hydrogen bonds with target sequence ends. **Extension:** DNA polymerase (Taq) adds nucleotides to the 3' end of each primer to synthesize new strands.
2.  Ideal primers are **18-28 nucleotides long**, have a **50-60% GC composition**, lack long strings of a single base, and are **not self-complementary**.
3.  Taq polymerase is **heat-stable**, meaning it remains functional even at the high temperatures (up to 95°C) required for DNA denaturation. This allows the PCR cycle to be repeated multiple times without adding new enzymes.
4.  PCR is used to **amplify very small amounts of DNA** found at crime scenes (from hair or blood spots) into a sufficient quantity for accurate testing and forensic detection.
5.  Researchers use **expression vectors**, which are cloning vectors containing a **highly active bacterial promoter** upstream of the insertion site. This helps the bacterial host recognize the gene and express it in the correct reading frame.
