This lecture, titled **"DNA Tools & Biotechnology-V"** and delivered by Prof. Sanjeeva Srivastava at IIT Bombay, provides a detailed laboratory demonstration of the processes involved in studying gene expression, specifically focusing on RNA extraction and complementary DNA (cDNA) synthesis.

### **1. Introduction to Gene Expression Analysis**

The lecture focuses on how to study gene expression patterns in a laboratory setting.

  * **cDNA Synthesis:** This is the generation of **complementary DNA (cDNA)** from an RNA template through a process called **reverse transcription**. *Complementary DNA* is a synthetic DNA version of an RNA molecule, which is more stable for analysis.
  * **Measurement:** By measuring cDNA levels, researchers can access and quantify the levels of expression of specific genes.
  * **Amplification:** The resulting cDNA can be used directly as a template for the **Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)**, a technique used to make many copies of a specific DNA segment.

### **2. Lab Demonstration: RNA Extraction**

The first step in cloning a human eukaryotic gene is isolating RNA from a clinical sample, such as human whole blood.

  * **Surface Preparation:** Unlike DNA, RNA is a very unstable and **labile** (easily broken down) biomolecule. To prevent degradation, all surfaces, pipettes, and tubes must be cleaned with **RNaseZAP**. *RNaseZAP* is a chemical that destroys RNases, which are enzymes that naturally degrade RNA.
  * **The Phenol-Chloroform Protocol:** This is the specific method used for extraction.
      * **Cell Lysis:** 200 microliters of whole blood is mixed with **TRIzol Reagent**. *TRIzol* contains phenol and **guanidine thiocyanate**, chemicals that **lyse** (burst open) the cells to release their contents into the solution.
      * **Phase Separation:** **Chloroform** is added to the mixture. After centrifugation (spinning at high speed), an **aqueous layer** (a clear, water-based top layer) forms containing the separated RNA.
      * **Precipitation and Pelleting:** The sample is incubated overnight in **isopropanol** and then centrifuged again at high speed to "pellet down" the RNA. A **pellet** is a small solid mass of concentrated material at the bottom of a tube after spinning. The resulting RNA pellet is then dissolved in sterile distilled water.

### **3. Quantification of Extracted RNA**

Once isolated, the RNA must be quantified to determine its concentration.

  * **Spectrophotometry:** Quantification is done using a spectrophotometric reading at **260 nanometers (nm)**.
  * **Blanking:** Because the RNA is dissolved in distilled water, water is used as a **blank** (a reference sample containing no RNA) to calibrate the instrument.
  * **uDrop Plate:** A **uDrop plate** is used in a **Multiskan GO instrument**, allowing for readings at 260 and 280 nm using very small amounts of the sample.
  * **Calculation:** The concentration is calculated using a specific formula: **Concentration (at 260 nm) = A260 \* 40µg/ml \* Dilution Factor**. An absorbance reading (**A260**) of 1 corresponds to approximately 40 micrograms per milliliter of single-stranded RNA.

### **4. Removal of Genomic DNA Contamination**

Isolated RNA often contains contaminating genomic DNA, which must be removed before cDNA synthesis.

  * **DNase Treatment:** Approximately 1 microgram of the quantified RNA is treated with the enzyme **DNase I**. *DNase* is an enzyme that specifically degrades DNA while leaving RNA intact.
  * **Incubation:** A buffer is added to increase enzyme activity, and the reaction is incubated at 37 degrees Celsius for 30 minutes in a **dry bath**.
  * **EDTA Addition:** 1 microliter of 50 millimolar **EDTA** is added. *EDTA* is a **chelating agent** that **sequesters** (binds and removes) divalent cations, which prevents the RNA from **hydrolyzing** (breaking down with water) during later heating steps.

### **5. cDNA Synthesis Procedure**

The final stage is converting the purified RNA template into cDNA.

  * **Reaction Components:** The reaction mixture includes:
      * **OligodT Primer:** A stretch of thymine nucleotides that binds to the **poly A tail** (a long chain of adenine nucleotides) found at the end of most mRNA molecules.
      * **Reaction Buffer:** Used to maintain optimal chemical conditions.
      * **RiboLock RNase Inhibitor:** Prevents any remaining RNase from degrading the RNA template.
      * **10mM dNTPs:** These are the building blocks (deoxynucleotide triphosphates) used to synthesize the new DNA strand.
      * **Reverse Transcriptase (RT) Enzyme:** The key enzyme that performs the actual conversion of RNA into cDNA.
  * **Experimental Controls:** Three controls are essential for validating the results:
    1.  **No Reverse Transcriptase Control:** Contains all components except the RT enzyme to test for any remaining genomic DNA contamination.
    2.  **No Template Control:** Contains all components except the RNA template to test for contamination in the reagents themselves.
    3.  **Positive Control:** Uses a provided control RNA as a template to ensure the kit and procedure are working correctly.
  * **Incubation:** The final reaction is incubated at 42 degrees Celsius for one hour.

### **6. Conclusion and Outcome**

The outcome of this specific demonstration was the successful isolation of RNA and the synthesis of cDNA, which was then used to amplify the **GAPDH region** of the cDNA. **GAPDH** is a "housekeeping gene" frequently used as a standard for comparison in expression studies. Once synthesized, cDNA is stable and can be stored for long periods for future PCR experiments.

#### **Short Question Solutions**

1.  RNA is a very **unstable and labile** biomolecule compared to DNA. It degrades easily, requiring all surfaces and equipment to be treated with RNase inhibitors (like RNaseZAP) to prevent enzymatic breakdown.
2.  Concentration is calculated at 260 nm using the formula: **Concentration = A260 \* 40 µg/ml \* Dilution Factor**. This is based on the standard that an absorbance reading (A260) of 1 corresponds to approximately 40 µg/ml of single-stranded RNA.
3.  Reverse Transcriptase is the key enzyme that **converts the RNA template into complementary DNA (cDNA)**. This allows for gene expression levels to be quantified and stabilized for further PCR experiments.
4.  The "No Reverse Transcriptase" control contains all reaction components except the RT enzyme. Its purpose is to **test for any residual genomic DNA contamination** that might be present in the sample, as any amplification in this control would indicate DNA was not fully removed.
5.  An OligodT primer is a **stretch of thymine nucleotides**. It is used because it specifically **binds to the poly A tail** found at the end of eukaryotic mRNA, ensuring that the messenger RNA is the primary template for cDNA synthesis.
