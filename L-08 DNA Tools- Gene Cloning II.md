This lecture provides a step-by-step laboratory demonstration of **DNA cloning**, specifically focusing on the workflow of molecular cloning to introduce a foreign gene into an organism.

### **The DNA Cloning Workflow**

The process involves several precise steps conducted under sterile conditions to ensure successful gene insertion and selection.

#### **Step 1: Preparation and Restriction Digestion**

  * **Vector and Insert:** The foreign gene of interest (the **insert**) is introduced into a carrier molecule called a **vector**, which is typically a bacterial **plasmid**.
  * **Restriction Enzymes:** Both the vector and insert are treated with **restriction enzymes** (such as **EcoR1**) to cut the DNA and create **compatible ends**.
  * **Reaction Conditions:** This digestion cocktail includes the DNA, a specific restriction enzyme buffer, water, and the enzyme itself. The mixture is incubated at **37°C** for one hour, which is the optimum temperature for the enzymes to act.

#### **Step 2: Ligation**

  * **Joining DNA:** The cleaved vector and insert are joined using a **ligase enzyme**.
  * **Ligation Mixture:** This mixture contains the vector DNA, insert DNA, ligation buffer, and the ligase enzyme.
  * **Incubation:** After a short vortex and spin, the ligation mixture is incubated at **room temperature for three hours**. This results in a covalently closed recombinant DNA molecule.

#### **Step 3: Transformation**

  * **Host Cells:** The ligated DNA is introduced into host cells, such as **DH5 alpha**, which lack restriction enzymes that might destroy foreign DNA.
  * **Aseptic Conditions:** This step is performed in a **laminar hood** using UV light for 10 minutes beforehand to ensure extremely sterile conditions.
  * **Heat Shock Treatment:** The ligation mix is added to **competent cells** (host cells) and incubated on ice for 30 minutes. They then receive a **heat shock treatment** at **42°C for 2 minutes**, which helps the foreign DNA enter the host cell.

#### **Step 4: Plating and Selection**

  * **Plating:** The transformed cells are spread onto **LB agar plates** using a sterile spreader in circular motions.
  * **Antibiotic Selection:** The media contains an antibiotic, such as **ampicillin**. Because the plasmid vector contains an **ampicillin resistance gene**, only host cells that have successfully taken up the vector will grow into colonies.
  * **Incubation:** Plates are incubated upside down at **37°C overnight** (approximately 16 hours).

#### **Step 5: Verification and Inoculation**

  * **Positive Clones:** Successful transformation results in visible bacterial colonies on the plate.
  * **Differentiation:** Researchers must confirm if the colonies contain the **recombinant molecule** (vector + insert) or just a **self-ligated vector**.
  * **Inoculation:** A single colony is picked and added to liquid **LB media** containing the appropriate antibiotic.
  * **Final Growth:** The culture is kept in a **shaking incubator** at 37°C overnight. A successful culture will appear **turbid** (cloudy), indicating significant bacterial growth.

While the basic steps are straightforward, achieving a correct clone often requires months of **parameter optimization** and verification to ensure the sequence is accurate and not a self-ligated vector.

### Solutions

#### MCQ Solutions

1.  **C) 37°C**
2.  **B) To confirm there is no contamination in the experiment**
3.  **B) It lacks restriction enzymes that would destroy foreign DNA**
4.  **C) 10 minutes**
5.  **B) To help the foreign DNA enter the host cell**
6.  **C) Ampicillin**
7.  **C) 3 hours**
8.  **B) To kill any existing microorganisms on it**
9.  **A) To prevent contamination from the lid's loose ends**
10. **C) Turbid (cloudy)**

#### Short-Answer Solutions

1.  The cocktail consists of the DNA (vector or insert), a specific restriction enzyme buffer, water to make up the volume, and the restriction enzyme (e.g., EcoR1).
2.  A **recombinant molecule** contains both the vector and the inserted foreign gene, while a **self-ligated vector** is a vector that has closed back on itself without incorporating the insert.
3.  Performing the reaction on ice ensures that the enzymes remain stable and do not start acting prematurely before the proper incubation period at their optimum temperature.
4.  Spreading is complete when the sample is spread uniformly across the agar, the surface starts to look dry, and the researcher feels friction against the spreader.
5.  The shaking motion provides aeration and ensures the bacteria have access to nutrients, leading to proper growth indicated by a turbid culture.
